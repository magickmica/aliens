The Great Moon Pie Mystery

The missing dessert that vanished without a crumb.

Orbit Sector 9, Galactic Archive

In the summer of 1987, one of the universe's most beloved desserts vanished without warning.

Known simply as The Great Moon Pie, the colossal confection measured nearly 700 miles across and served as both a tourist attraction and navigation landmark for dessert travelers. Then, overnight, it disappeared.

Official records describe a triple-layer marshmallow cream pie topped with crystallized stardust sugar and a buttery comet-crust.

No debris. No crumbs. No explanation.

Witnesses reported seeing flashes of glowing whipped cream light moments before the pie vanished from orbit. Thirty-nine years later, investigators are still searching for answers.

Breaking Science

The Secret Life of Rainbow Sherbet

New evidence suggests sherbet may be alive.

For centuries, rainbow sherbet has delighted beings across the galaxy. But recent studies suggest it may be doing more than simply tasting delicious.

Researchers at the Institute of Frozen Curiosities conducted a six-month experiment involving positive affirmations and flavored desserts. The results were unexpected.

When complimented, samples of rainbow sherbet became brighter, softer, and produced sparkling sugar particles. When criticized, they became duller and occasionally generated tiny weather systems.

Scientists remain divided. Some insist the findings are evidence of consciousness. Others argue the sherbet is simply reacting to environmental conditions.

The sherbet itself has declined all interview requests.

Why Aliens Invented Sprinkles

Archaeologists uncover sweet evidence from the dawn of civilization.

A recent excavation beneath the Frost Rings of Saturn uncovered fossilized rainbow sprinkles dating back nearly four million years. The discovery suggests ancient civilizations may have used sprinkles as currency, ceremonial offerings, and possibly even interplanetary navigation tools.

"We found entire temples built around giant sprinkle formations." — Professor Blip Zargon, Cosmic Culinary Institute

Researchers are now working to decode mysterious sprinkle patterns believed to contain messages from an extinct dessert culture. One thing is certain: the universe has always loved a little extra sparkle.
